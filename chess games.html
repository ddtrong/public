Chess Games

Sun Herald

Sunday January 15, 1995

IAN ROGERS

IN ONE of the most amazing results on record, Dinh Duc Trong, 17, of Vietnam, has won the Australian Open in Perth after missing the first three games.

Dinh was delayed in Vietnam by visa problems and arrived three days after the tournament had started.

Organisers generously allowed Dinh to compete, giving him the equivalent of three draws for the first three rounds. Less generously, but quite fairly, he was then paired as if he was among the leaders to avoid any benefit from his late arrival (ie, weaker opposition).

Even so, Dinh had only eight rounds left to overhaul most of the 99-player field. He conceded a fifth round draw to the early leader, Adelaide's Ngan Koshnitsky, then won his last six games to finish half a point ahead of top seed Alex Wohl.

Wohl had taken the lead by winning a critical seventh round game against Koshnitsky but fell back into a tie for first when he was stopped two rounds later by Dinh (the game is given below). Demoralised, Wohl then drew with Melbourne junior Sam Low to give Dinh a crucial half point break.

Vietnamese-born Koshnitsky, who had started with four consecutive wins, faded to finish tied for 13th.

Dinh and Koshnitsky are the latest in a line of talented young players, including Dao and Tu, coming from a country which joined the World Chess Federation less than a decade ago.

Vietnam finished only half a point behind Australia at the recent Chess Olympiad in Moscow and almost 50 places above Australia at the Women's Olympiad. There seems little doubt Vietnam has now joined Australia, the Philippines and Indonesia as a dominant force in the Asian-Pacific zone.

AUSTRALIAN OPEN, Perth, 1994/5 White: Dinh Duc Trong. Black: Alex Wohl. Opening: English.

1.c4 e5 2.Nc3 Nc6 3.g3 g6 4.Bg2 Bg7 5.e3 d6 6.Nge2 Nge7 7.d4 exd4 8. exd4 O-O 9.O-O Bf5?! This square should be kept free for Black's knight on e7.

10. Be3 a6 11. Qd2 Re8 12. Rac1 Bd7?! 13.Bg Qb8? Black has played the entire opening incoherently but the text move is an eccentricity too far. The ugly 13...f6 was necessary.

14.Ne4! Kh8 15.Nf6 Rd8 16.d5 Ne5? On 16...Na7, 17.Nxh7 wins a pawn but unfortunately 16...Ne5 leads to the loss of even more material.

17.f4! Ng4 18.Nxg4 Bxg4 19.Bxe7 Re8 (See Diagram) 20.h3! Bxe2 21.Qxe2 Perhaps Wohl had believed he could exploit the pin along the e file but his poorly placed king prevents this; on 21...Bf8 or 21...Bf6, the reply 22.Bf6 is check. Black should now try 21...Kg8 22.Rf3 Bf6 23.Re3 Bd4 although White will untangle and win after 24.Kh2 Bxe3 25.Qxe3 followed by Re1, Qh6 and Bf6.

21...Qc8 22.Rce1 Qd7 23.Rf3 Kg8 24.Kh2! Now White will be able to play 25.Re3 without facing the pin ...Bd4.

24...b5 25.Re3 h6 26.b3 b4 27.Bh4 Rxe3 28.Qxe3 Bf8 29.Bf6 With a clear piece less and an inferior position, Wohl should resign the game.

The top seed probably could not yet accept the disaster which had befallen him.

29...a5 30.Bb2 Bg7 31.Bxg7 Kxg7 32.c5 Qf5 33.Qe7 a4 34.cxd6 cxd6 35.Qxd6 axb3 36.axb3 Qc2 37.Qxb4 Ra2 38.Qe4 Qxb3 39.d6 Rd2 40.Qe5+ Kh7 41.Re2 Rd1 42.f5 gxf5 43.Qxf5+ Kg7 44.Qg4+ Kf8 45.Re8+! 1-0 Black is finally convinced by 45...Kxe8 46.Qc8 checkmate.
